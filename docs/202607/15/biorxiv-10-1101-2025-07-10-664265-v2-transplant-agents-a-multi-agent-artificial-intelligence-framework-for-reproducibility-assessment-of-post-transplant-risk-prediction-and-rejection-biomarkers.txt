Abstract 14 

Reproducible biomarker identification and transplant rejection risk prediction remain 15 

fundamental yet unsolved challenges in transplantation medicine. Traditional approaches rely on 16 

hypothesis-driven analyses and domain expertise, limiting scalability and generalizability across 17 

diverse populations. We introduce Transplant-Agents , a data-driven multi-agent AI framework 18 

integrating large language models (LLMs) with machine learning algorithms for automated 19 

biomarker identification and rejection risk prediction. Agents interact through structured, 20 

iterative dialogue governed by predefined rules and criteria, converging on optimal biomarker 21 

sets reproducible across multiple iterations. We evaluated three multicenter clinical trial 22 

transplant datasets from ImmPort, comprising 683 patients across kidney, liver, and heart 23 

transplant cohorts. Transplant-Agents achieve AUROC scores of 0.93, 0.88, and 0.88. Feature 24 

importance analysis further confirms the stability, interpretability and potential generalizability 25 

of identified biomarkers. This work demonstrates that AI-agent frameworks can reliably 26 

reproduce established transplant biomarkers while enabling transparent, validated, and 27 

standardized risk prediction pipelines. 28

The integration of artificial intelligence into clinical decision-making has transformative 31

fundamental challenge in biomedical AI. This challenge is particularly acute in organ 33 

transplantation, where risk stratification directly impacts patient outcomes and resource 34

Methods 85 

Data pre-processing. We used longitudinal data from three CTOT consortium studies—CTOT-86 

01 (kidney), CTOT-14 (liver), and CTOT-05 (heart)—comprising 683 patients across U.S. and 87 

Canadian sites, obtained from the ImmPort portal 14 . Each study included static variables 88 

(demographics, medical history) and longitudinal variables (clinical and molecular 89 

measurements), with post-transplant rejection as the primary prediction endpoint. Only 90 

longitudinal measurements collected before the rejection event were used as inputs, summarized 91 

into minimum, maximum, and most recent values per time series. These summary features, 92 

combined with the static variables, were used to predict rejection and identify associated salient 93 

variables. Variables with over 30% missing data were excluded; remaining missing entries were 94 

imputed with zeros. 95 

Framework Overview. In typical biomedical scenarios, including experiment and clinical trials, 96 

data collection typically comprises a combination of static and longitudinal data. Static data 97 

usually comes from demographic information, medical history, and baseline clinical information. 98 

Longitudinal data such as laboratory tests and transplant-specific clinical tests are collected at 99 

multiple time-points during pateint follow-up at pre- and post-transplantation. We employed a 100 

simple yet effective pre-processing feature engineering method to derive the maximum, 101 

minimum, and latest values from longitudinal variables and concatenated them with static 102 

variables 16 . The processed variables serve as input into the Transplant-Agents framework as 103 

shown in Figure 1(a), which has two specialized agents on ML and domain expertise. The ML 104 

agent served as an ML engineer, and the domain expertise agent will serve as a domain expert, 105 

e.g., transplant clinician. The computational tools are available for ML agents, for example, 106 

LightGBM in our design and implementation, and the feature importance calculator, which 107 

calculates feature importance from the tree-based model (e.g., LightGBM). The ML agent calls 108 

the ML model to predict the risk of the primary target, e.g., transplant rejection. Then the feature 109 

importance calculator is called to rank the features. Through prompt learning, the ML engineer 110 

agent will make the decision to add or delete features to improve prediction performance based 111

Building agents from LLM. The AI agents are built from the LLM with prompt learning as 117 

shown in Figure 1(b) and Figure 4. We designed a ‘Role-Expertise-Task’ prompt template to 118 

build the specialized agent 17 . The ‘Role’ defines the character the agent will act in the task, e.g., 119 

ML engineer, domain expert, clinician, etc. The ‘Expertise’ specifies the skills or scientific 120 

expertise the agent will have, e.g., feature selection, model building, kidney transplant, etc. The 121 

‘Task’ indicates the goal of the agent, or the task the agent should complete, e.g., diagnose 122 

disease (prediction/classification task), adjust the input variable set to optimize diagnosis 123 

performance (feature selection task), analyze features from a clinical perspective (domain critic 124 

task), etc. 125 

For the ML engineer agent, we assign the ‘Role’ as ML engineer. The ‘Expertise’ of an ML 126 

engineer agent is building a machine learning pipeline and feature engineering, which are 127 

common ML skills. And the ‘Task’ is to diagnose the disease, optimize the diagnosis 128 

performance, and provide an interpretation of the results with XAI techniques. 129 

For the domain expert agent, we assign the ‘Role’ as the domain expert. The ‘Expertise’ of a 130 

domain expert can be kidney transplant, liver transplant, or heart transplant, etc. And the ‘Task’ is 131 

to check whether the input features are consistent with basic biomedicine knowledge and provide 132 

critical feedback. The details of the prompts we use are presented in Figure S1 of the 133 

supplementary file. 134 

Processing input data with agent. The input data consists of lab tests, biomarkers, demographic 135 

informatics, etc., which are categorical or numerical variables. Instead of directly using an LLM-136 

driven agent to process the raw data, including both variable names and values, we design a 137 

paradigm to separately intake names and values into the agent, as shown in Figure 4(a). The 138 

agent will only process the input variable names, which can leverage the strength of LLM to 139 

understand the semantic and clinical meanings of each variable. The agent will use ML tools to 140 

directly intake the variable values and evaluate the performance of the current feature set on the 141 

designated task. ML tools can be some ML models, e.g., LGBM, etc. The disentangling of 142 

variable names and values eases the burden of the agent, which can just use tools, do analysis 143 

like a ‘brain’. After the ML tool is called to process the input variables, the agent will intake and 144 

analyze the results and the feature importance from the ML model. 145 

Tools usage for task completion. For the diagnosis task, the agent utilizes ML models as the 146 

core predictive tool. These models are trained on labeled datasets to learn complex patterns and 147 

relationships between input features and target outcomes. Commonly used models may include 148 

decision trees, random forests, support vector machines, gradient boosting algorithms (e.g., 149

For the feature analysis task, the agent employs feature importance tools to evaluate the 152 

contribution of each feature to the model’s prediction performance. This includes both filter-153 

based methods (e.g., mutual information, correlation analysis), wrapper methods (e.g., recursive 154 

feature elimination), and model-based approaches. A typical tool for feature importance analysis 155 

is built-in feature importance from tree-based models, which quantitatively ranks features based 156 

on their impact on model accuracy or loss. 157 

For the interpretability and visualization task, the agent adopts SHAP (Shapley Additive 158 

explanations) value-based methods. The SHAP package provides a unified framework to explain 159 

individual predictions by attributing them to input features in a theoretically grounded way, 160 

based on cooperative game theory. This enables both global and local interpretability, allowing 161 

users to understand not only which features are important in general, but also how each feature 162 

contributes to specific predictions. 163 

Collaboration between agents. The collaboration between the two agents—namely, the ML 164 

engineer agent and the domain expert agent—is designed as an iterative discussion process that 165 

facilitates knowledge exchange and decision consensus. These agents engage in a dynamic 166 

dialogue where each contributes to its specialized expertise to refine the overall analysis and 167 

conclusions. 168 

Specifically, the ML engineer agent focuses on the technical aspects of model development, 169 

including feature engineering, model selection, and performance evaluation. Meanwhile, the 170 

domain expert agent provides critical contextual insights grounded in domain-specific 171 

knowledge, helping to validate the relevance and plausibility of model findings and feature 172 

interpretations. 173 

During the collaboration, the ML engineer agent presents model outputs, such as diagnostic 174 

predictions and selected features based on importance rankings. The domain expert agent then 175 

reviews these outputs, questioning or endorsing specific features or model behaviors based on 176 

domain understanding. If discrepancies or uncertainties arise, the agents exchange feedback 177 

iteratively, prompting model adjustments or re-examination of the data. 178 

This back-and-forth continues until both agents reach a consensus, harmonizing the technical 179 

rigor of machine learning with the nuanced understanding of domain expertise. This consensus-180 

driven approach ensures that the final diagnostic decisions or feature interpretations are both 181 

statistically sound and practically meaningful. 182 

As illustrated in Figure 1(c), this discussion method enhances the reliability and interpretability 183 

of the system’s outputs by leveraging complementary strengths of the two agents through 184 

cooperative interaction. Such a collaborative framework not only improves decision quality but 185 

also fosters transparency and trustworthiness in the deployment of AI-driven solutions. 186

Implementation for Transplant Rejection. The Transplant-Agents framework is leveraged for 187 

scalability and modularity, allowing it to adapt to biomarker identification and diagnostic tasks 188 

by reconfiguring its agentic goals and toolsets. We validated our approach using organ 189 

transplantation—a domain with an acute clinical demand for precise risk stratification and non-190 

invasive monitoring. Currently, clinicians and researchers prioritize the discovery of non-191 

invasive biomarkers to preemptively detect graft rejection and optimize post-transplant outcomes. 192 

Transplant-Agents extend these clinical physician capabilities by autonomously distilling high-193 

relevance biomarkers, architecting specialized diagnostic pipelines, and providing clinically 194 

grounded interpretations of the results. 195 

To evaluate the framework's ability to reproduce and identify clinical biomarkers, a primary 196 

objective was to predict transplant rejection by identifying critical non-invasive biomarkers from 197 

multi-modal inputs, including urine, blood, and vital signs, prior to the clinical onset of rejection. 198 

This aligns with the study goal of the original CTOT trials: transitioning from invasive biopsies 199 

to reliable, non-invasive molecular and physiological diagnostics. Transplant-Agents achieve this 200 

complex objective through a structured four-stage workflow, ensuring the fidelity of the 201 

identified biomarkers relative to original clinical trial findings. 202

pivotal role in constructing a robust machine learning pipeline tailored for diagnosing post-204 

transplant rejection. This agent, designed with expertise in developing diagnostic ML pipelines, 205 

leverages structured input features to ensure precise and reliable outcomes. For Task 1, the agent 206 

is responsible for training and testing a machine learning model using patient lab tests and 207 

supplementary clinical data. This process involves preprocessing the data, selecting appropriate 208 

algorithms, and evaluating model performance to accurately identify instances of post-transplant 209 

rejection. For Task 2, the agent focuses on optimizing the input variables by employing 210 

computational techniques to identify and prioritize high-relevance biomarkers. This optimization 211 

enhances the model's diagnostic accuracy by reducing noise and focusing on the most impactful 212 

features, ultimately improving the reliability of the diagnostic process. 213

Second, we build the transplant expert agent, which provides clinical feedback for the ML 217

ML engineer and transplant expert discussions 225 

The ML engineer agent and the transplant expert agent collaborate through multiple rounds of 226 

discussions, fostering an iterative process where the ML engineer proposes selected biomarkers, 227

Compared with expert-designed scores. As shown in Figure 2 (a-1), (d-1), compared to clinical 347 

scoring criteria, Transplant-Agents achieve an AUROC of 0.93. As many kidney transplant 348 

rejections are subclinical, it is not surprising that the blood urea nitrogen (BUN) score has an 349 

AUROC of 0.62 and the estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR 9), has an AUROC of 0.43. 350 

On the other hand, human expert-designed scores such as eGFR, KDPI, and EPTS exhibited low 351

Transplant-Agents Accelerate Biomarker Identification 420 

As shown in Figure 3, we present the feature importance of rejection diagnosis of three organ 421 

types. The transplant expert agent automatically analyzes the important biomarkers and features 422 

from the clinical perspective, as shown in Figure 5. Supported by the Transplant-Agents , we 423 

analyze the salient features of kidney, liver, and heart transplant rejection from the medical 424 

aspect as follows. 425 

Biomarkers for heart transplant rejection. As shown in Figure 3(a), we identified novel 426 

clinical associations and supported by previously reported insights from scientific literature. 427 

Higher β-glucuronidase levels were associated with lower rejection risk, a finding not previously 428 

described. Although the mechanism remains unclear, β-glucuronidase is involved in 429 

glycosaminoglycan turnover and inflammatory pathways, suggesting potential indirect effects on 430 

tissue repair and alloimmune activity. This signal warrants validation in independent cohorts. In 431 

contrast, elevated white blood cell (WBC) counts were associated with an increased risk of 432 

rejection, consistent with the role of T cells and monocytes/macrophages in mounting immune 433 

responses against the donor heart 33 . This immune activation promotes cytokine release, tissue 434 

injury, and the generation of donor-specific antibodies, thereby driving both acute and chronic 435 

rejection. Monitoring WBC levels of post-transplant is therefore clinically important 34 . Similarly, 436 

a greater degree of Class II recipient HLA mismatch correlated with higher rejection risk, 437 

reflecting enhanced CD4  T cell activation and donor-specific antibody formation that 438 

contribute to antibody-mediated rejection and chronic complications such as cardiac allograft 439 

vasculopathy 35 . Reduced alpha-1-microglobulin (A1M) levels also appeared to increase rejection 440 

risk. Although direct evidence is limited 36,37 , A1M has antioxidant, cytoprotective, and anti-441 

inflammatory properties, and lower levels may diminish protection against immune-mediated 442 

tissue injury. Finally, several additional factors have been implicated in heart transplant rejection, 443 

including BK virus 38,39 , Fas ligand 40 , and blood pressure 41 , all of which are consistent with 444 

prior reports. 445 

Biomarkers for liver transplant rejection. As shown in Figure 3(b), extending this analysis to 446 

liver transplantation, we identified both previously reported and potential newly biomarkers that 447 

haven't been covered in previous literatures, supported where possible by published studies. One 448 

novel observation was that lower serum potassium levels may be associated with increased 449 

rejection risk. While hyperkalemia is well recognized in liver transplantation 42 , reduced 450 

potassium levels have not been reported in this context. Our data-driven finding raises the 451 

possibility of a new biomarker that warrants confirmation in follow-up studies. In contrast, 452 

sodium and creatinine are well-established factors in liver transplant outcomes, consistent with 453 

their inclusion in the MELD-Na score 4,24 , which also demonstrated competitive diagnostic 454 

performance for liver rejection in our analysis (Table 2). Lower albumin levels may also indicate 455

Biomarkers for kidney transplant rejection . As shown in Figure 3(c), we summarized clinical 461 

insights on kidney transplant rejection supported by prior literature. A lower red blood cell count 462 

was associated with an increased risk of rejection, consistent with the high prevalence of anemia 463 

after kidney transplant due to immunosuppression and chronic kidney dysfunction 49 . Elevated 464 

urinary CCL5 (RANTES) expression indicated a higher risk of rejection, reflecting its role in 465 

recruiting T cells, macrophages, and other immune cells; prior studies have validated CCL5 as an 466 

important non-invasive biomarker 50,51 . Similarly, higher urinary CXCL9 expression was 467 

strongly associated with rejection. CXCL9, induced by interferon-γ, mediates T cell recruitment 468 

and has consistently been identified as a reliable non-invasive biomarker of rejection 52–54 . Low 469 

serum bicarbonate was also linked to greater rejection risk. While not a direct marker of rejection, 470 

reduced bicarbonate reflects metabolic acidosis and impaired kidney function, which often 471 

accompany rejection 55 . Additional factors with strong evidence of association include 472 

hematocrit 56 , CD8 expression 57,58 , and CCR1 expression 59,60 .473 

Discussion 474 

Solid organ transplantation represents a life-saving intervention for thousands of patients 475 

annually, yet transplant rejection remains a leading cause of graft failure and patient mortality. 476 

There is an emergent need to identify non-invasive biomarkers contributing to post-transplant 477 

outcomes. Biomarker identification requires rigorous validation through wet lab experiments and 478 

clinical trials, and data-driven approaches offer a systematic pathway to prioritize candidates for 479 

downstream validation. Transplant-Agents integrates computational feature analysis with 480 

semantic reasoning to select rejection-relevant biomarkers, enabling both prediction and 481 

interpretation within a unified framework. 482 

From the quantitative results on rejection diagnosis, Transplant-Agents outperforms baseline 483 

methods, including traditional ML models and feature selection methods. Notably, Transplant-484 

Agents surpass expert-designed diagnosis rules significantly, suggesting that data-driven feature 485 

selection can capture rejection-associated patterns beyond those encoded in current clinical 486 

protocols. The use of SHAP values further enables attribution of individual feature contributions 487 

to model predictions, providing a transparent basis for biomarker prioritization and supporting 488 

downstream experimental validation. 489 

The limitations are our analysis was based on 683 patients from three CTOT studies, which 490 

limits statistical power and generalizability beyond this consortium; longitudinal variables were 491 

also summarized into minimum, maximum, and most recent values, which may not fully capture 492 

finer temporal dynamics. Additionally, SHAP-based attribution reflects statistical association 493
